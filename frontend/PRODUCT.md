# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Players at a hackathon venue in India who scan a QR code on a TV screen and join instantly from their own phones — mid-range Androids on Chrome, over venue Wi-Fi or mobile data. They span ages and tech comfort; many are multilingual speakers of Assamese, Bengali, Hindi and other Indian languages. Their context is a noisy, crowded hall: standing, one-handed, glancing at the screen at arm's length between conversations. The job to be done is simple — join in seconds, play a fast voice-guessing round with a stranger, win points, feel good about it. A secondary audience watches the venue TV leaderboard and decides whether to walk over and scan the QR.

## Product Purpose

Dialect Data Factory turns dialect speech collection into a live party game. Paired players describe picture cards aloud in their native language while their partner guesses the meaning — every validated round quietly produces a clean, human-verified training sample for a dialect speech corpus. Success looks like a hall full of people playing voluntarily and having fun, a steadily climbing clip counter on the TV, and a corpus no one had to be paid or begged to produce.

## Positioning

Your language is your superpower — speak it, score with it, and have fun doing it. Dialect pride and friendly competition in one move: the game is won *by* speaking the language you grew up with, and every screen reinforces that speaking it is both the point and the play.

## Brand Personality

Warm, witty, competitive. The voice is a friendly rival at a festival game stall: it teases, it cheers, it keeps score, and it never talks down. Reference feels to steal deliberately: Kahoot's fat colored answer tiles and everyone-in-the-room lobby energy; Duolingo's juicy correct/wrong feedback moments, points fly-ups, and encouraging failure states; HQ Trivia's live-event tension — timers and the sense that something synchronous is happening right now.

## Anti-references

Not generic AI-startup slop: no purple-blue gradients, glassmorphism, sparkle emojis, or "AI magic" framing — the AI angle is earned, not decorated. Not childish edtech: no infantilizing mascots or primary-school aesthetics; players are adults at a tech event. Not a corporate dashboard: no gray cards, tiny type, or data-dense layouts in the player's face — the metrics live on the TV ticker. Not Western stock-photo minimalism: sterile whitespace-heavy minimal clashes with the culturally rooted card imagery.

## Design Principles

The server is the source of truth: the client renders state and sends actions; a screen never invents, predicts, or caches game logic — most critically, the card label must not exist anywhere on the speaking screen until the server sends it.

One glance, one action: every screen is readable at arm's length in a noisy hall and has exactly one primary thing to do, sized for a thumb.

Celebrate the voice, not the machine: feedback moments honor the player's language and their contribution; the corpus-building story stays in the background as pride ("your voice, understood"), never as data-extraction framing.

Live-event tension: timers, the queue, and the leaderboard should make the game feel synchronous and communal — something happening in this room, right now.

Warm failure: re-records, wrong guesses, and unclear rounds get playful, blame-free copy that keeps players in the loop instead of pushing them out.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Venue-pragmatic WCAG AA: body text at ≥4.5:1 contrast, touch targets ≥48px, large type throughout, fully usable one-handed. No sound-dependent cues except the core audio-guessing mechanic itself. Devanagari and Bengali–Assamese scripts must render at full size and quality wherever game content appears.
